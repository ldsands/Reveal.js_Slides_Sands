Background

  • Experts and Expert Performance (Ericsson et al. 2006)
    1. Experts have accumulated knowledge
    2. Experts have specialized mental skills
    3. Experts have applicable experience through targeted practice
    4. Usually some form of restriction becoming a member of a profession, occupation, guild, or obtaining a credential from a respected institution
    5. Expert performance do not transfer to other areas

Background

  • Lay Expertise (Prior 2003)
    1. No consensus on what defines a Lay expert
    2. Most research in lay expertise comes from medical sociology and epidemiology
    3. Lay expertise gained through personal experience
    4. Learning the language is typical of lay experts
    5. Engaged in advocacy AIDS, breast cancer (Barker 2011, Epstein 1995)

Background

  • The Dunning-Kruger Effect (Dunning 2011)
    1. The less one knows about something the more errors they will make
    2. Lack of knowledge prevents seeing their errors made
    3. The faster someone obtains an answer the more confident they are about those answers

Background

  • Self-Efficacy (Ross and Mirowsky 2013)
    1. Self-efficacy is a belief of the amount of control you have over your life
    2. Education is the best way to increase self-efficacy

Putting it together

  • There are more lay experts then the literature has indicated
    1. Lay expertise gained through personal experience
      • The only person who truly knows what the illness is like is the patient, only they know how it feels, affects their daily routines, relationships, etc. (Zola 1973)
    2. Engaged in advocacy
      • People share their own health recommendations based on their own health experiences within their social networks (Liu, King and Bearman 2010)

Putting it together (cont.)

  • The Internet (through easily digestible information) is making more lay experts who experience Dunning-Kruger
    1. Learning the language is typical of lay experts
      • Sites like WebMD, Wikipedia, Mayo Clinic, etc. provide an easy way to learn the language
    2. The faster someone obtains an answer the more confident they are about those answers
      • It is very fast and seems uncomplicated to look up something on Google/Bing then to do the research necessary to understand a medical condition
    3. The move toward consumerism (ranking, ratings, etc.) in the medical field is leading to an attitude of ‘customer knows best’ when interacting with medical professionals (Hardey 1999)

Conceptual Definitions

  1. Experts and Expert Performance
  2. Lay Expertise
  3. The Dunning-Kruger Effect

Research Question

  • Is confidence in the understanding of information obtained affected by the source of the information?
  • Is confidence in the understanding of information obtained affected by the simplicity of the information?

Hypotheses

  1. There will be an increase in confidence in understanding of information the simpler the information presented
  2. There will be an increase in confidence in understanding of information when that information is on the Internet

Experimental Setup

  • IV - Source of information, Format of information
  • DV - Confidence in their understanding of the information, Retention of the information
  • Deception - The duration of time it takes to forget recently learned information (will learn for 10 min then wait 10 min before tested)
  • Manipulation - Three groups all given 10 minutes to learn as much as they can about hypohidrotic ectodermal dysplasia from the source given to them
    1. Source will be a textbook entry about hypohidrotic ectodermal dysplasia
    2. Source will be the textbook entry made to look like Wikipedia
    3. Source will be the Wikipedia entry on hypohidrotic ectodermal dysplasia

Experimental Procedure

  • Participants will take a pretest asking about their experience with medical/genetic information (classes, work experience, etc.) and their confidence in understanding difficult material quickly
  • Participants will be randomly assigned to one of three groups with different sources of information
  • After 10 minutes of trying to learn as much information as possible they will wait 10 minutes during which they will take a test asking them how well they think they retained the information
  • 10 minutes after the material has been taken they will take a information retention test
  • A debriefing will then occur
