20 February, 2020

Levi Sands

Lim, Alwyn and Kiyoteru Tsutsui. 2012. “Globalization and Commitment in Corporate Social Responsibility: Cross-National Analyses of Institutional and Political-Economy Effects.” American Sociological Review 77(1):69–98.

Background

Corporate Social Responsibility

  • CSR frameworks (guidelines or standards) meant to incentivize companies to "do good"
  • They have been growing very fast in the last couple of decades
  • Two main CSR frameworks discussed here:
    • The UN Global Compact (GC)
    • The Global Reporting Initiative (GRI)

The UN Global Compact (GC)

  • The GC is meant to encourage self-reporting business activities in a variety of areas (see image on next slide)
  • There are "minimal requirements" associated with membership (see pg. 72)
  • Due to reporting being voluntary many can join but then not submit a report (these reports are called "Communications on Progress")
    • Companies that report to the GC are classified as meeting having "baseline commitment"
    • Those who join the GC but do not report are classified as having "ceremonial commitment"

The Global Reporting Initiative (GRI)

  • The GRI is much more restrictive and thus in order to be a member not only is reporting mandatory but it is also much more extensive that what is required by the GC
  • Those who are members of the GRI are classified as having "substantive commitment"

Hypothesis 1:

The more a country is connected to international society, whether through governmental or nongovernmental linkages, the more likely its government and corporations will participate in global CSR frameworks.

  • IV: ↑ connections to global community (non-economic)
  • DV: ↑ companies doing CSR
  • BECAUSE: "civil society is a major source of world society pressure" (see pg. 77)

Hypothesis 2:

The greater the legitimacy of CSR principles in a country and the more rationalized the business environment, the more likely its government and corporations will participate in global CSR frameworks.

  • IV: ↑ rationalized businesses (transparent)
  • DV: ↑ companies doing CSR
  • BECAUSE: "citizens [...] are more likely to [...] hold businesses accountable for their actions" (see pg. 78-79)

Hypothesis 3:

Cross-national economic relations, through either short- or long-term transactions, exert pressure on governments and corporations in developing countries to participate in global CSR frameworks.

  • IV: ↑ long-term AND/OR short-term connections to global community (economic; in developing countries)
  • DV: ↑ companies doing CSR
  • BECAUSE:
    • long-term due to "greater business embeddedness in the host country" (see pg. 79)
    • short-term due to need for signals of good business partner (see pg.79)

Hypothesis 4:

The more liberal a national economic system, the more likely the government and corporations in a country will participate in global CSR frameworks.

  • IV: ↑ neoliberal economy
  • DV: ↑ companies doing CSR
  • BECAUSE: to try to defect accountability (paraphrased from pg. 79)

Hypothesis 5:

5a: In developing countries, participation in global CSR frameworks will likely be ceremonial.

  • IV: in developing countries, participation will be mostly ceremonial
  • DV: companies say they're doing CSR (but not really in practice)
  • BECAUSE: lack of capacity to implement standards (see pg. 80)

5b: In developed countries, participation in global CSR frameworks will likely be ceremonial.

  • IV: in developed countries, participation will be mostly ceremonial
  • DV: companies say they're doing CSR (but not really in practice)
  • BECAUSE: "organized hypocrisy" (see pg. 80)

Methods Notes (Data from 2000 to 2007)

  • DV: Launch of GC in a country
  • DV: Number of companies in the GC along with their participation in reporting (out of total domestic countries)
  • IV: Number of NGOs
  • IV: Level of education (for level of citizen pressure for CSR)
  • IV: Country transparency
  • IV: Bilateral export context and bilateral investment context (based on how many GC companies operating in country through investment or importing from the country)
  • IV: National economic system (i.e. how left or right the country is from: World Bank’s Database of Political Institutions)
  • CVs: Number of companies, and GDP per capita

Methods Notes cont.

  • For event history analysis: semiparametric Cox proportional hazards model (or just Cox proportional hazards model)
    • "Survival models relate the time that passes, before some event occurs, to one or more covariates that may be associated with that quantity of time." (See Wikipedia for more)

Methods Notes cont.

  • For the number of cooperations participating: negative binomial regression
  • 27 OECD countries are the "developed countries" and 72 non OECD countries for "developing countries"

Results Summarized

  • Hypothesis 1 received partial support (global pressure increases CSR adoption for all CSR except ceremonial)
  • Hypothesis 2 received no support
  • Hypothesis 3 received support for the short-term economic investment but not for long-term (for government support and substantive support)
  • Hypothesis 4 received support (for baseline and ceremonial)
  • Hypothesis 5a received no support
  • Hypothesis 5b received received support (suggesting organized hypocrisy in developed countries)
