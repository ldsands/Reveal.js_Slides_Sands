Theory

  • "a supposition or a system of ideas intended to explain something, especially one based on general principles independent of the thing to be explained" source
    • Sociologists answer lots of questions but sometimes they find correlations that they didn't expect
    • We will be identifying aspects of these theories in everyday life

Structural Functionalism: Functional Structures Persist

  • Society has needs structures form to fill those needs
  • The example often used is a living organism
  • Limitations and criticisms
    • Function is a value judgement e.g. sports
    • Integration in systems mean less freedom e.g. military, religious organizations, education

Functionalism Example

Health Care in the US

  1. Doctors do a majority of the diagnosis work
  2. Due to a lack of doctors nurse practitioners and PAs have increased in number to make up for the lack of doctors
  3. Just because this is how it happened doesn't mean it is the only way to fill a need
  4. Insurance in the US is privatized and largely managed through employers
  5. This is not the only way of getting insurance that works look at other countries such as Canada

Conflict Theory

  1. Started with Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels
    1. Observed bad conditions in factories in the 1840 England
    2. Concluded revolt of the workers
    3. Didn't foresee the gains made by unions and government intervention on behalf of workers
  2. In-group vs out-group (us vs them)
    1. e.g. Iowa vs Iowa State, Gonzaga vs St. Mary's
  3. Limitations and criticisms
    1. Frequently ignore commonalities and consensus

Conflict Theory Example

  1. Utah State, Utah and BYU in the formation of the WAC
  2. Utah State in the early 60s was beating Utah and BYU in football
  3. This lined up with the formation of the WAC because Utah and BYU had more money they lobbied to leave Utah State out of the new conference
  4. They did this so Utah State would cease its climb in popularity, it worked really well

Symbolic Interaction

  1. Grew out of backlash against early Psychology (stimulus response)
  2. Mead/Blumer added interpretation to stimulus response
  3. Cooley the "Looking-glass self" (mirror) we se ourselves how others see us (based upon other's actions toward us)
  4. Use symbols to interact
    • Uniforms (suit and tie, military ranks, my shirt, etc.)
  5. Limitations and criticisms
    • does little to address inequality, overly focused on symbols
