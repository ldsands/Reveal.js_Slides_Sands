## The Study of Drugs in Society - To study drugs scientifically, avoid labeling them as good or bad, instead observe their effects - Drugs as so important in society that they are hard to categorize - More than 90% of American adults use drugs - Drug Control Categories: - Over-the-counter: caffeine, nicotine, alcohol, aspirin - Controlled Substances - Prescription: antibiotics, codeine - Illegal: heroin, cocaine - In 2024, marijuana is an over-the-counter drug in many states and a prescription drug in many more states - Weed is still illegal in 14 states and banned nationwide by the federal government
## Medical, Recreational or Performance Enhancing? - **Functional Drug Use Categories**: Recreational drugs, Medical drugs, Performance enhancing drugs - Recreational drugs: Alcohol, caffeine, nicotine, cocaine, marijuana, morphine, meth - Medical drugs: morphine, marijuana, antibiotics, steroids, meth - Performance enhancing drugs: steroids, Ritalin, caffeine, nicotine, marijuana, cocaine, meth - Most drugs are used for more than one reason - Effective drugs are usually good and bad at the same time - **Drug Abuse As Function of Medical Advances** - The history of drug abuse is tied to the acceleration of medical advances that began in the 1800s - Medical history has many examples of new wonder drugs used to counteract the problems caused by earlier drugs but that then cause even greater individual and social problems - **Morphine/Heroine/Cocaine example**
## Morphine: Medical Miracle and Addictive Curse Howard Markel, The Anatomy of Addiction, 2011 - 1803 Morphine developed from opium begins to be used as a tonic mixed with alcohol - Morphine is a miracle drug used to treat pain and reduce the shock of surgery and traumatic injury - 1850 The hypodermic syringe becomes available. Many doctors become addicted to morphine - 1865 The first American opioid crisis occurred when Civil War veterans became addicted after being wounded in battle - Morphine "rush:" constricted pupils, warmth and erotic sensations soon followed by several hours of oblivion - Morphine side effects: constipation, nausea, suppressed breathing, overdose death
## The Problem with Morphine - Morphine use creates tolerance - Regular users need larger doses to get the rush and become physically addicted - Withdrawal symptoms: restlessness, irritability, depression, anxiety, insomnia, cramps, diarrhea, muscle aches - 1898 Heroin, a powerful variant of morphine, at first used to treat morphine addiction but found to be more dangerous
## Using Cocaine to Treat Morphine Addiction - Cocaine was developed in 1855 from the coca plant. Used to treat morphine addiction. No physical withdrawal symptoms. - Native South Americans chewed coca leaves to increase endurance, withstand pain, feel happy in hardship. - 1860s Vin Mariani: wine laced with coca extract. - 1886 Coca Cola is made with coca extract until 1903. - 1880s Sigmund Freud researches cocaine as a cure for his friend's morphine addiction. - Cocaine effects: Immediate sense of delight and confidence. Thoughts and ideas flow. Rapid speech. Surge of energy and motivation. Hunger vanishes. Sexual desire increases.
## Morphine to Heroine to Cocaine - These "wonder drugs" led to terrible outcomes - To treat the addiction caused by previous "wonder drug" a new one was created
## Meth Goes to War - 1930s Benzedrine amphetamine pills popular in Great Depression - American, German, Japanese, and British armies all gave meth tablets to their soldiers during World War II - Adolf Hitler was almost certainly a meth addict - Methedrine-brand meth was widely popular in the 1960s for weight loss and depression - Housewives and their children used it regularly - 1967 Doctors wrote 31 million meth prescriptions - Millions more bottles of meth were legally sold in Mexico and re-imported to the U.S. illegally - 1970 US Bureau of Narcotics (now DEA) classified meth as a dangerous drug - Limited production, tracked doctors' scripts - 1972 Meth use had fallen dramatically (90% in 5 years)
## Meth's Resurgence - 1972 Meth had become a minor drug problem - 1972 Cocaine abuse had suddenly become a major drug problem - Drug smugglers filled the demand for stimulant drugs - Ephedrine the main ingredient in meth became available as an over-the-counter cold medicine - Users could make their own meth - Recently FDA recommended no more phenylephrine (doesn't work) - Heartland problems make meth attractive: financial and psychological - Lori Arnold the "Meth Queen of Iowa" located in Ottumwa - Reding, Nick. 2009. Methland: The Death and Life of an American Small Town published by Bloombsury Press. - Good paying jobs in industry were vanishing replaced by low-paying jobs that required long hours - Meth helped - Meth use appeals to people struggling with the American Dream - Weber's Protestant Ethic - It was widely accepted for 80 years until almost 1980
## Strain Theory of the American Dream - Robert Merton's **Strain Theory of Deviance**: The American Dream causes stress that pushes people to deviate - Strain occurs because most people want to be rich and successful but relatively few can be - Strain produces four kinds of deviant people: - **Ritualists** who unhappily do their legitimate jobs but no longer believe it serves any useful purpose - **Retreatists** who drop out of society (hippies of the 1960s for example, "turn on, tune in, drop out") - **Rebels** who seek to bring down society (terrorist bombers for example) - **Innovators** who find new and unapproved ways to succeed - Lori Arnold and Roland Jarvis (her husband) are Innovators in their pursuit of the American Dream
## Drug Controversies and Demonization - **Moral Panic**: "Heightened fear and concern, misplaced anxiety, a reviled agent responsible for conditions less harmful than we think" (Goode and Ben-Yehuda 2009) - The social construction of drug "epidemics" (Mosher and Akins 2014) - Are all drug "epidemics" dangerous or are some moral panics? - Marijuana - 1800s to around 1930 mostly portrayed as benign especially when compared with alcohol - The Federal Bureau of Narcotics (now the DEA) "initiated a vigorous anti-marijuana propaganda campaign" (Mosher and Akins 2014) - Included movies (Marijuana Girl, Refer Madness) - The backlash to marijuana was a direct result to the increasing Mexican immigration > From 1900 to 1934 (just prior to the passage of the Marijuana Tax Act in 1937), most articles on the topic asserted that the primary users of marijuana were members of minority groups—in particular, Mexicans. For example, one commentator from Sacramento, California, noted, “marijuana, perhaps now the most insidious of narcotics, is a direct by-product of Mexican immigration. . . . Mexican peddlers have been caught distributing sample marijuana cigarettes to schoolchildren” (cited in Musto, 1999, p. 220) > > (Mosher and Akins 2014)

## Successful Treatment for Addiction: Try, Try Again - Schachter's Research on quitting smoking (Sghachter 1982) - Success rates for drug abuse patients during their second and third treatments remain above 20%.
