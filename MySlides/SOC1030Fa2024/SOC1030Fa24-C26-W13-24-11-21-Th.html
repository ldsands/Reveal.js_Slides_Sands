## Myths Oddities and Misconceptions Around US Government and Founding - Myth: US was founded as a Christian Nation (Fea 2016) - Reality: many of the founders were deists, the founding documents are very agnostic in what little religious language there is - Myth(ish): The US is the oldest democracy (Foner 2019) - Reality: The constitution was a replacement adopted in 1787 for the Articles of Confederation - Reality: The civil war/reconstruction is sometimes referred to as the second founding because of the drastic changes made to the constitution and other aspects of government - Is not a complete replacement and a civil war where the system was drastically changed failures of the US system of democracy? - Myth: The separation of church and state was created at the founding (Pew Research Center 2009) - Reality: Some states has a state church, the separation of church and state developed over time - Misconception: The founders wanted a 2 party system (Drutman 2020) - The founders explicitly designed a system to prevent any political parties from forming - They were terrified of what two party systems do - 1792 first party formed (only 5 years after the constitution is adopted) - These myths are perpetuated for many reasons - One reason is to stop change to the system as it exists today - Some of these are just not well known
## How Rights Went Wrong (Greene 2021) - **Binary Approach to Rights**: The United States - When the rights of one person or group come into conflict with another on must ALWAYS win - Increases the stakes for every conflict between rights - **Proportionality Approach to Rights**: Germany, Canada, UK and most others - When rights come in conflict the judge decides using the circumstances of the conflict - Allows for recognizing many more rights - Example: Abortion > "In 1975, West Germany's constitutional court heard that country's own momentous abortion case at nearly the same time as Roe, approaching it from a startlingly different perspective. Abortion was illegal in almost all circumstances until the Social Democrats tried to pass a decriminalization law in 1974. The question before the West German court was not whether restrictions on abortion infringed the rights of women but rather whether liberalizing abortion interfered with the rights of fetuses. The court struck down the law for this reason. But rather than structure abortion jurisprudence around the rights of either women or fetuses alone, the court has consistently structured the law around both sets of rights." > > "In practice, this has meant that the German government must take seriously both the constitutional value of women's autonomy and its commitment to fetal life. There, as in the United States, the best way to protect fetal life is not to outlaw abortion (which only drives the practice underground) but rather to provide women with meaningful alternatives to termination." (Greene 2021) - Decriminalized abortion for first trimester only but are very easy to obtain - Government provides for free or cheap many services and other incentives to make abortions less common - There isn't anything explicitly stated in law that says that all rights when they come in conflict HAVE to have one winner

## Three Examples of Gun Control - Australia ([RAND](https://www.rand.org/research/gun-policy/analysis/essays/1996-national-firearms-agreement.html) and [Wendover](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v0aGGOK4kAM)) - 1996 Port Arthur Massacre - Banned most types of semi-automatic rifles in 12 days - Not the first attempt (1987) - New Zealand ([PBS](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/nation/why-the-u-s-and-new-zealands-responses-to-mass-shootings-are-so-different) and [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gun_law_in_New_Zealand)) - 2019 Christchruch Mosque Shootings - Banned all military rifles and many other kinds of guns in 28 days - United States ([AP](https://apnews.com/article/congress-gun-violence-bill-live-updates-20f7c9738fee4eba1d7fed155301ee25)) - First federal gun reform passed 2022 first time in decades
## Why This Example? - Not really about gun laws - All three had strong culture of gun ownership - All three had mass shootings - All three had strong support to change the laws - All about policy and the ability to implement it - Three different systems with a different number of veto points
## Veto Points in Government - **Veto Point**: the place in government where policy can be rejected - New Zealand (1 veto point) - Unicameral (Prime minister is part of the parliament) - That's it no other veto points - Same system: Finland, Greece, Luxembourg, Portugal, Sweden, Denmark, Iceland, Norway - Similar but different systems with one veto point: UK, Ireland, Austria, France - Australia (3 veto points) - Bicameral, both chambers have to pass a law (some more complicated veto ability by the cantons or member states) - Very similar to Switzerland - (Hammond 2003; Stepan and Linz 2011)
## Veto Points in Government: US - 4 veto points - Bicameral (both have to pass law) - House needs simple majority - Senate usually needs 2/3 to pass (filibuster) - Presidential veto - Supreme Court (Judicial Review see Marbury v Madison) - Amendments are even harder to pass - 3/4 of state legislatures need to ratify - The more veto points the less things change - How equal were people at the founding? - (Hammond 2003; Stepan and Linz 2011)
