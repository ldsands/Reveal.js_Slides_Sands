## Institutional Racism - **Institutional racism** (**Systemic racism**) is a form of racism that is embedded as normal practice within society or an organization - Stokely Carmichael and Charles Hamilton in 1960s - Harder to identify and therefore less often condemned by society (because no one is to blame) - Examples: Schools, banks, redlining and discrimination in housing market, biased law enforcement policies
## Implicit Racial Bias - **Prejudice**: the individual biases and belief systems that attribute characteristics to a group of people so that we prejudge group members - **Racial discrimination**: the differential treatment of people based on their race - **Implicit racial bias**: the subconscious attitudes and stereotypes that individuals hold about people of different racial or ethnic backgrounds, often without conscious awareness - No prejudice, but with discrimination - Example: Implicit Association Tests - Example: the Shooter Bias Study
## Implicit Association Tests - Implicit Association Tests [https://implicit.harvard.edu/](https://implicit.harvard.edu/) - Assessment intended to detect subconscious associations between mental representations of objects in memory - The speed determines how comfortable or uncomfortable an idea is to the test taker
## The Shooter Bias Study - The Shooter Bias Study (Correll et al. 2002) - Joshua Correll, a social psychologist - Racial bias in perceptions of threat and decision-making - Law enforcement and simulated shooting scenarios. - Participants in the study played a video game, in which the goal is to shoot people who had a gun, but not to shoot unarmed players
## The Shooter Bias Study - The Shooter Bias Study (Correll et al. 2002) - Participants are more likely - To mistakenly identify unarmed Black individuals as armed - To shoot them more quickly than White individuals - This bias occurs even when the race of the participant and the race of the target are matched - Training can make a difference
## Statistical Discrimination - **Statistical discrimination**: - A situation in which an individual (e.g. employer) makes a decision about an individual on the basis of beliefs about the average characteristics of a social category rather than the characteristics of the specific individual - Why does this happen? - It is less costly to do decide based on stereotypes, usually not because of an dislike of people in that category - Example: Employers believe that on average a young black man will be a less reliable employee than a young white man with the same formal qualifications, and since it is difficult to get precise information about individual reliability, the employer will rely on presumed group traits to make the choice - Graph below: Callbacks (Pager 2003)
## Color-Blind Attitudes - Color-blind attitudes refer to the belief that race should not be a factor in how individuals are treated and that everyone should be treated equally regardless of race - Should we embrace a color-blind attitudes towards social practices in work place, social encounters, and public policies?
## "Color-Blind Racism" (Bonilla-Silva) - Color-Blind Racism is the view that racial inequality is perpetuated by a supposedly color-blind stance that ends up reinforcing historical and contemporary inequalities, disparate impact, and institutional bias by “ignoring” them in favor of a technically neutral approach - "race neutral" rhetoric relies more on culture and nationality to explain differences between nonwhites and whites than immutable physical traits

Color-Blind Attitude (Coleman Hughes)

  • Adopt a color-blind attitude, but at the same use class-based policy to redistribute resources and opportunities for the poor in which people of color are over-represented
