## Sociology and Network Science - Sociological Origins of Network Science - Early theory by Georg Simmel and Emile Durkheim - Some work done with operationalization in the 30s and 50s - In the 1970s developed into a science by Mark Granovetter and several others - Today some (relatively) famous Sociologists (Duncan J. ## Social Networks (Network Science) - **Nodes**: the distinct element or actor - The "dots" in network graphs - **Ties or Edges**: the connections between nodes - The lines connecting nodes - **Hubs**: Nodes with a high degree - Users with many ties (friends) also called bridging ties - Nicholas A. Christakis and sickness on campus - **Small-World**: "Easy" to get from any node to every other node in the network - The average degree of separation between 2 random nodes was 4.7 - Source: The Anatomy of the Facebook Social Graph - **Clustering**: to what degree the network is sorted into clusters - Facebook high clustering due to recommendation algorithm - How are clustering and social closure related? - **Homophily**: to what extent the ties are between nodes that are similar to each other - Example: "Mormonverse" on Reddit: automatic banned on r/lds if user has ever posted on r/exmormon

## Polarization - **Issue-Based Sorting**: "Democrats hold liberal issue positions and Republicans hold conservative issue positions." (Mason 2018) - **Issue-Based Polarization**: "[...] is closer to the traditional understanding of the term polarization, and indicates an increasing distance between the average issue positions of Democrats and Republicans." (Mason 2018) - **Social Sorting**: "increasing social homogeneity within each party, such that religious, racial, and ideological divides tend to line up along partisan lines." (Mason 2018) - Democrats are increasingly irreligious, a member of a minority racial group, and liberal - Republicans are increasingly religious, white (the most numerous racial group), and conservative - **Social Polarization**: "[...] defined by prejudice, anger, and activism on behalf of that prejudice and anger." (Mason 2018) - "American partisans today are prone to stereotyping, prejudice, and emotional volatility, a phenomenon that I refer to as social polarization." (Mason 2018) - We may be undergoing another major shift in the parties right now but it is still to early to tell for sure

## Is Social Media Polarizing - **Illusory Truth Effect**: "[...] tendency to believe false information to be correct after repeated exposure" ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illusory_truth_effect)) - **Continued Influence Effect**: "[...] the tendency for misinformation to continue influencing people's thinking and decision-making even after it has been corrected or debunked." ([Jason Hreha](https://www.thebehavioralscientist.com/glossary/continued-influence-effect)) - **Confirmation Bias**: the tendency to look for, prefer, interpret, and remember information that confirms their current beliefs (Nickerson 1998) - **Cognitive Dissonance**: "the mental disturbance people feel when they realize their cognitions and actions are inconsistent or contradictory" [(Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognitive_dissonance) - **Motivated Reasoning**: uses emotionally biased reasoning to produce justifications or make decisions that are most desired rather than those that accurately reflect the evidence, while still reducing cognitive dissonance - **Echo Chamber**: a media environment in which information and beliefs that confirm prior beliefs are amplified - [They're lying to you. Here's what to do about it. - Johnny Harris](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yWgG3Mgn2Gc) (Required video)
Illusory Truth Effect and Continued Influence Effect, both concepts influence what information we use to develop our beliefs
Confirmation bias impacts what information we integrate into or reinforce our beliefs

Cognitive dissonance and motivated reasoning are key to understanding why people will defend their beliefs even (and perhaps especially) in the face on contradictory evidence

All these concepts are encouraged on social media platforms, creating echo chambers which encourage polarization
