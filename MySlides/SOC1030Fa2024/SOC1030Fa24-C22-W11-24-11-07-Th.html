## Tragedy of the Commons - Unregulated grazing on common land in Great Britain and Ireland -- William Forster Lloyd (1833) - "Tragedy of the commons" - [Garrett Hardin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garrett_Hardin) (1968) - Governing the Commons - [Elinor Ostrom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elinor_Ostrom) (1990) - Who has rights to access and use resources? - How much of the resources can be withdrawn? - Who will monitor whether the resource is being used according to the set of rules? - How to punish those who break the rules? - Who will decide what the rules are? - What to do in case of a disagreement? - Rather than privatization of the common resources, or controlled and monitored by the State, people can often self-organize themselves in order to successfully manage their resources by *creating a whole system of social institutions*.
## Hardin's Theory vs Ostrom's Findings - Hardin's theory: - If each herdsman sharing a piece of common grazing land made the individually rational economic decision of increasing the number of cattle he keeps on the land, the collective effect would deplete or destroy the commons - Elinor Ostrom's findings: - She spent time with traditional communities whose life and economy largely dependent on a natural resource base - Switzerland and Japan (mountain meadows), Spain and Philippines (Irrigation system); Nepal (forest); Indonesia (Fisheries) - Ostrom finds that each of these communities have developed their own set of rules for determining how to use their natural resources - Disproved Hardin's theory by conducting field studies on how people in small, local communities manage shared natural resources

## Example: Fracking - Hydraulic fracturing - Fracking is the process of drilling down into the earth before a high-pressure water mixture is directed at the rock to release the gas inside. - Water, sand and chemicals are injected into the rock at high pressure which allows the gas to flow out to the head of the well. - The process can be carried out vertically or, more commonly, by drilling horizontally to the rock layer, which can create new pathways to release gas or used to extend existing channels.

## Negative Externalities of Environmental Problems - **Negative Externalities**: a negative side-effect of an activity that affects other people - Market efficiency depends on prices reflecting the true costs of producing things, but firms often systematically displace costs and risks on others in many ways - Example: a paper mill - Not incredibly sophisticated - Cost includes vegetable fibres, electricity, water, human resources - The paper factory's best financial interest is to dump the waste directly into a river - It is expensive to process the waste - Inland Empire Paper Company in Millwood, WA (just up the river from where I grew up) - This is the exact problem of NE, the cost of processing the wastes now is displaced on those living near by the river, those farmers, and residents. There are quite many social problems that involve NE, especially environmental problems; and later we will have in-depth discussion about NE displaced on racial minority communities. - Who can stop NE? ## A Special Case of Negative Externalities: NIMBY - NIMBY: **Not In My BackYard** - Displacing costs onto the vulnerable groups - Radioactive waste dumps - Halfway houses for the mentally ill - Fertilizer plants - Cancer causing air pollution [ProPublica](https://projects.propublica.org/toxmap/) - "In predominantly Black census tracts, the estimated cancer risk from toxic air pollution is more than double that of majority-white tracts." [ProPublica "Sacrifice Zones"](https://www.propublica.org/article/toxmap-poison-in-the-air) - Power is key to shifting negative externalities onto others - Corporations do their best to displace costs onto others - Affluent communities are better able to organize collective actions to block such displacement of cost - More examples: - Nitrogen fertilizer and farming - Acid rain - Amazon rainforest burning - Love Canal in New York
## Love Canal - Love Canal is an aborted canal project branching off of the Niagara River - In 1950s, the Hooker Chemical began using the partially dug canal as a chemical waste dump, around 21,000 tons of toxic chemicals - Hooker capped the 16-acre hazardous waste landfill in clay and sold the land to a School Board - A series of inexplicable illnesses - President Jimmy Carter declared a state of emergency in 1978 - Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act (CERCLA) in 1980 - Known as the Superfund - Holds responsible parties liable or when no viable party responsible - Charges chemical companies to help clean up hazardous waste - How might Loper Bright Enterprises v.
